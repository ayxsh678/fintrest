# Fintrest — Brand Direction

*Premium finance, quietly confident. Three distinct directions — pick the one that sounds the most like the company you want to build.*

---

## Positioning Read

Before the marks: the brief says *simple like Apple, trustworthy like a bank, smart like AI*. The failure mode of most Gen-Z fintech is loud color + playful mascot + "we're the cool one." That does not age into a billion-dollar brand. Cash App did, but only because it owned green so early nothing else could.

The winning move for Fintrest is **editorial restraint** — the visual language of money that has been managed for a long time (private banking, rare watches, architectural magazines) retooled for the phone. Monarch, Mercury, Copilot.money, Rally, and Stripe already understand this. The work is to land a mark that feels like it belongs on that shelf, not on the App Store trending list.

The name itself is a gift — "Fintrest" contains **crest** (a peak, a heraldic mark), **interest** (the engine of wealth), **trust**, and **rest** (peace of mind). Good marks should compound one of those, not ignore them.

---

## Concept 01 — **The Crest**

*The most poetic of the three. Best if Fintrest wants to feel like the New Yorker of finance apps — editorial, quietly luxurious, for people who want money to be a background hum, not a foreground hobby.*

### The idea
A single, asymmetric curve that reads as the silhouette of a wave frozen at its crest — the moment just before it breaks. Pulled from the etymology inside the name itself ("crest" = peak of a wave, top of a helm, heraldic mark). The viewer sees an abstract mark first, then — about a second later — the connection to the name clicks. That delayed aha is the whole point; it's what separates premium marks from literal ones.

### Why it's intelligent, not obvious
It isn't a chart line, an arrow, or a growth curve. Those all point to the *future* — Fintrest's mark points to *the peak moment itself*. The curve is drawn with a subtle mass shift (thicker at the apex, tapering to a hairline tail) so it has the physics of water, not the stiffness of a graph. If you rotated it 90° it would look like a serif's entry stroke — that's intentional.

### Typography
- **Wordmark:** GT Super Display (or Canela Deck as a substitute) — high-contrast serif, lowercase "fintrest," tight tracking. Editorial gravitas without going full heritage bank.
- **UI body:** Söhne — the quiet Swiss sans used by The New Yorker and Mailchimp's premium work. Reads as expensive without effort.
- **Numbers:** GT America Mono, tabular figures. Your balance is the hero; it needs to sit still.

### Palette
| Role | Name | Hex | Use |
|------|------|------|-----|
| Primary ink | Midnight Slate | `#0E1116` | Backgrounds, headings |
| Primary light | Paper | `#F5F1EA` | Cards, surfaces (warm, not white) |
| Accent | Vault Gold | `#C9A86B` | The mark, key actions — muted, antique, not bling |
| Supporting | Tide | `#2A4B5C` | Charts, secondary surfaces |
| Alert | Ember | `#B24A2A` | Reserved for losses / warnings only |

## Concept 02 — **The Ledger Line**

*The most architectural. Best if Fintrest wants to feel like an institution — the fintech equivalent of a Swiss private bank's first app. Most defensible long-term; hardest to execute.*

### The idea
Strip a logo down until only what is necessary survives. The mark is two strokes: one long horizontal line, one short vertical stroke meeting it just off-center. It's the most primitive accounting notation in human history — the tally, the ledger line, the "this is where the books balance" — abstracted until it reads as pure form. The "F" is only *implied*; you won't see it unless someone tells you.

### Why it's intelligent, not obvious
It references finance's oldest tool (the double-entry ledger, Venice, 1494) without drawing anything you'd find in a stock image search. It's the opposite of a mascot: it's architecture. Marks this austere age well — they look like they were designed in 1985 or 2035, and nobody can tell. Compare to Chase's octagon or Bank of America's banner: they are carried by consistency, not cleverness. The Ledger Line plays the same long game.

### Typography
- **Wordmark:** Editorial New by Pangram Pangram — a modern display serif with just enough stroke contrast to feel like an architectural drawing label. Set "FINTREST" in small caps.
- **UI body:** Inter Display, weight 400, generous line height. Disappears into the page.
- **Numbers:** GT America Mono, always right-aligned in tables.

### Palette
| Role | Name | Hex | Use |
|------|------|------|-----|
| Primary ink | Obsidian | `#0A0A0B` | Everything is on this, or out of this |
| Primary light | Bone | `#EDE6D6` | Warm off-white, never pure `#FFF` |
| Accent | Peacock | `#0E3B43` | The mark, primary buttons |
| Supporting | Stone | `#8A8276` | Secondary text, dividers |
| Alert | Oxblood | `#5B1F1F` | Losses, destructive actions |

## Concept 03 — **The Aperture**

*The most contemporary. Best if Fintrest wants to sit next to Linear, Raycast, and Notion on a designer's home screen. Most "Gen-Z premium."*

### The idea
The mark is a stylized camera-aperture — not fully open, not fully closed — built from 4 or 5 identical geometric blades around a center void. It reads as a lens, an eye, or a quiet pinwheel depending on how long you look. The metaphor is **attention**: Fintrest isn't selling you more data, it's sharpening your focus on the few numbers that actually matter. "Smart insights" visualized as a mechanical act of seeing.

### Why it's intelligent, not obvious
An aperture is a tool for *controlling how much light gets in* — exactly what a good personal-finance app does with information. It's borrowed from the language of premium photography brands (Leica, Hasselblad) rather than finance, which makes it sit in an unusual shelf. Rotational symmetry also means it animates beautifully on splash screens and loading states — the blades can open/close as a loading indicator without feeling like a spinner.

### Typography
- **Wordmark:** Neue Haas Grotesk Display, weight 55 Roman, lowercase "fintrest," -2% tracking. The quietest of the three. Pure Apple/Linear energy.
- **UI body:** Neue Haas Grotesk Text, 16px default.
- **Numbers:** JetBrains Mono or Berkeley Mono — mono for readouts gives a "terminal of your money" feel that Gen Z reads as trustworthy (think Mercury, Copilot).

### Palette
| Role | Name | Hex | Use |
|------|------|------|-----|
| Primary ink | Carbon | `#0F0F10` | Dark-mode default background |
| Primary light | Pearl | `#FAFAF7` | Light-mode surfaces |
| Accent | Evergreen | `#1F5C46` | The mark, CTAs — a deep, *considered* green (not Robinhood, not crypto) |
| Supporting | Mist | `#A6ADB4` | Secondary text |
| Highlight | Bone White | `#F2EFE6` | Hover states, inner glow |

## App Icon — The Most Important Artifact

*Tested against the Gen-Z iPhone home screen, not against the App Store.*

The icons have to survive next to Spotify, Instagram, and Apple's own apps at 60×60px. Rules I'm designing to:

1. **One dominant form. No wordmark, no two-letter monogram.** (Cash App, X, Threads — all won by committing to one shape.)
2. **A contained gradient, not a flat fill.** Pure flat looks cheap at small sizes; an over-gradient looks crypto. The target is a *whisper* of depth — like a spot of light hitting a ceramic surface.
3. **Dark-backgrounded icons always beat light ones on a busy home screen.** They read as premium, and they let the accent color pop.

### Concept 01 — Crest icon
Vault gold crest mark, centered, on a midnight slate background with a subtle radial vignette (as if lit from the top-left by a single window). The gold has a faint brushed-metal micro-texture — invisible at a glance, adds richness at 3× retina. Feels like the watch face of a mechanical chronograph.

### Concept 02 — Ledger Line icon
Obsidian background, bone-colored ledger mark, centered, sized at ~55% of the icon. A second, almost-imperceptible bone line runs edge-to-edge behind the mark, three pixels thick — the "page" the ledger is written on. Extreme restraint. Looks like a page from a private-bank statement.

### Concept 03 — Aperture icon
Near-black (`#0F0F10`) background with a faint inner glow of evergreen behind the aperture — the impression that there's light on the far side of the lens. The aperture blades are done in Pearl `#FAFAF7`, each blade with a 1° inward bevel so they catch shape. At small sizes it reads as a flower or an eye; at large sizes it resolves as a lens. Animated app-open state: blades pull open by 15° in a 300ms ease-out.

### My pick for the icon
If Fintrest wants to own a shelf for 10 years, **Concept 02 (Ledger Line)**. If it wants to be loved by designers in year one, **Concept 03 (Aperture)**. Concept 01 is the safer middle.

## Brand Direction (Visual Identity)

### UI vibe
- **Dark mode first.** Set the product against Carbon/Obsidian by default — Gen-Z finance apps that feel like Linear and Arc beat the ones that feel like Mint. Light mode should exist but feel like the "print version" of the dark mode.
- **Generous whitespace** — Apple-grade, 24-32px gutters on mobile. Money apps that cram data feel panicked; Fintrest should feel like it knows something you don't.
- **Numbers as heroes.** Every screen has one number 2-3× larger than anything else on it. That number is always tabular-figure monospace. Everything else is small, calm, secondary.
- **Serif accent labels.** Section labels ("Portfolio," "This Week," "Insights") set in a serif, small-caps, with 100-200 letterspacing, sitting above sans-serif body. Moves the product an entire tier upmarket for almost zero effort.
- **No illustrations of people, no emoji, no confetti.** Moments of delight come from *motion and sound*, not color explosions. Think the "click" of an AirPods case.

### Motion
- Default easing: `cubic-bezier(0.4, 0, 0.2, 1)` but on the slow side (280-320ms).
- Numbers animate on change — a quick odometer roll for balance changes.
- No bouncy physics. Nothing should spring.

### Gradients (use sparingly)
- **Halo gradient** for hero cards: radial, accent color at 20% opacity → transparent, origin off-canvas top-right. Gives cards a "lit from somewhere" quality without looking like a wallpaper.
- **No full-screen gradients ever.** That is the #1 tell of a crypto app.

### Photography / imagery
- If you use imagery at all: black-and-white editorial stills, shot in natural light. Objects over people. A single coffee cup on a wooden desk beats a stock photo of a smiling person every time.
- Better: **no photography, just typography and data**, like Mercury's site.

## Design Style References (the shelf Fintrest belongs on)

| Reference | What to borrow | What to *not* borrow |
|-----------|----------------|----------------------|
| **Mercury** | Dark UI, serif section labels, tabular numbers, the quiet confidence | Their banking-specific copy voice |
| **Copilot.money** | Monochrome palette with one accent, category pills, respect for whitespace | They are a bit too pastel — go darker |
| **Monarch Money** | Calm chart treatment, hierarchy of numbers | They over-rely on gradient color on charts |
| **Stripe** | The restraint, the motion, the way they treat code & numbers as beautiful | They're B2B; you need more warmth |
| **Linear** | The monospace-adjacent number treatment, command-K everywhere vibe | Their icon system is extremely them — don't copy |
| **Rally.co** | Editorial serif + sans pairing, premium feel without heritage-bank stuffiness | Their photography is expensive; you can skip it |
| **Anthropic / Are.na** | Serif in an AI/tech context | Their palettes are very specific — take the principle not the pixels |

Do **not** look at: Robinhood, eToro, any "coin" branded product, any neo-bank from 2019, any fintech using purple gradients.

---

## What I'd Ship First

1. **Pick Concept 02 or 03.** Concept 01 is beautiful but the serif wordmark costs you in the App Store listing where you're competing for a tap.
2. **Build the app icon before the full identity.** The icon is 70% of first impressions; everything else can iterate.
3. **Commit to dark mode and tabular numbers in the first build.** Those two decisions alone will make Fintrest look two funding rounds ahead of where it actually is.
4. **Skip the brand film and the hero illustrations for v1.** A static site with the wordmark, the icon, and one animated number hitting a new high is more powerful than an 'explainer' video.
